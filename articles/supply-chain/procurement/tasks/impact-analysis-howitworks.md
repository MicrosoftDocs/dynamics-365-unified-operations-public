# How impact analysis works #

This article explains how impact analysis determines whether a purchase order line change has downstream impact. It runs automatically based on configured sources, but can also be manually initiated.

It covers the impact of the following purchase order line changes: 

- Delayed delivery date (“Confirmed Delivery Date”)
- Quantity decreases
- Cancellations (where quantity is changed to 0)

**Note:** The following changes are recognized when communicated via e-mail by supplier communications, but do not trigger an impact analysis:
- Unit of measure
- Price
- Quantity increases
- Earlier delivery dates

## How impact is determined

Impact analysis evaluates planning and inventory data to classify changes as:
- **Has impact** - The change delays downstream orders and/or causes inventory levels to fall below minimum requirements
- **No impact** - The change does not delay downstream orders or cause inventory to drop below minimum levels

A change **Has impact** if it affects downstream orders and/or inventory levels.

 **Downstream orders in scope for the impact analysis:**
- **Sales orders** - Customer orders that may be delayed or cannot be fulfilled
- **Production orders** - Manufacturing orders that depend on the supply
- **Transfer orders** - Transfers that rely on the item
- **Forecast orders** - Demand forecast requirements used by the planning system to anticipate and prepare for expected future demand
- **Safety stock requirements** - Planned requirements generated by the planning system to maintain minimum inventory levels. These appear when the planning system has created planned orders to replenish safety stock, and the purchase order change affects the ability to fulfill those planned requirements.

### “Has impact” scenarios
Impact analysis flags impact when:
- **Downstream orders will be delayed** - The supply date moves after the delivery date required by connected demand
- **Quantity reductions affect coverage** - The reduced supply quantity is insufficient to cover downstream demand
- **Inventory threshold breaches** - The change causes inventory levels to drop below critical thresholds:
 	 - **Below minimum** - On-hand inventory falls below the configured safety stock level (but remains positive)
 	 - **Negative (stockout)** - On-hand inventory goes below zero and orders cannot be fulfilled, resulting in a complete stockout

### “No impact” scenarios
A change is classified as having no impact when:
- **No downstream orders found** - The purchase order is not linked to any dependent demand
- **Sufficient buffer exists** - Inventory levels and timing provide adequate buffer to absorb the change
- **Change is an improvement** - The purchase order date moves earlier or quantity increases (positive changes don't flag impact)

 ### Planning data used for the impact analysis

Impact analysis works regardless of whether Dynamics 365 planning systems (Master Planning or Planning Optimization) or external planning systems are used. What differs is how impact analysis traces the changed purchase order through the available planning data to find connected downstream demand. 

If using external planning systems, impact analysis traces downstream orders based on explicitly defined relationships (markings) only. 

If using a Dynamics planning system, impact analysis also follows the supply-demand chain through pegging relationships in the active dynamic plan to find which planned and confirmed orders depend on this changed supply. With peggings, it will be able to trace dependency chains such as purchase order → production order → sales order. Pegging enables broader, multi‑level impact tracing by traversing dependency chains created by planning. As a result, impact analysis can identify a broader set of affected orders, including indirect dependencies that are not explicitly marked. When Dynamics 365 planning systems are used, the available planning data is richer, which enables deeper and broader impact visibility.
